Case study 06 · Payments & POS

Payment gateway at Sysco LABS (CAKE POS)

Owned the CAKE POS payment gateway product as Proxy Product Owner from 2017–2019, building on 5 years in operations and support on the same product. The clearest example of the operator-then-builder move: the support data I generated became the product team's prioritization.

+120%payment gateway revenue ($40M)
2×payment gateway users

The foundation

Before owning the product, I spent five years in operations and support on CAKE POS - progressing from Application Support Engineer providing tier 1 and 2 support to Operations Lead on the payment gateway, building the support team from scratch. That tier-level visibility into how the product actually failed in the field generated the data that grounded the product team's prioritization. When I moved into the Proxy Product Owner role, I wasn't starting from a roadmap; I was starting from years of knowing exactly where the product broke.

Growing the gateway

As Proxy Product Owner I owned the CAKE POS payment gateway end-to-end: identifying issues through support data, coordinating with third-party processors, and shipping improvements. Over the role I doubled the number of payment gateway users and grew revenue 120% ($40M).

Fixing the chargeback workflow

The clearest example of how operations fed product: I studied how the chargeback process actually worked, traced the root cause to a broken investigation workflow, and rebuilt the tool with engineering to fix it. The result was $50,000/month in savings - a problem that was only visible because I'd lived in the support data first.

Why this still matters

Seven years in payments and POS - gateway revenue, third-party processor coordination, and chargeback investigation - is a genuine differentiator for B2B fintech. It maps directly to payments infrastructure, embedded finance, and the operational workflows that sit underneath B2B lending and SME platforms.

What I learned

  • Support data is product gold. The chargeback fix and the gateway growth both came from patterns only visible to someone who'd worked the tickets - not from a roadmap workshop.
  • Operate first, then build. This is where the signature move started: do the operational work long enough to see the structural problem, then build the system that removes it.
  • Payments rewards diligence. Money movement, processor coordination, and dispute handling punish hand-waving - the discipline carries into how I scope AI features today.
